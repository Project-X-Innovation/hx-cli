## 1. The Structural Gap

### The Problem Nobody Else Is Solving

On one side are ERPs. They provide a system that makes sense of the messiness of business — baked-in financial rules, accounting rules, and operational structures that businesses do not want to reinvent from scratch. That is the value of the ERP. It is a general system for handling real business complexity.

But every real business also has its own flows, its own exceptions, its own logic, and its own customizations. That is not an edge case. That is normal. Every business has customizations.

ERPs are not in the business of owning those customizations over time. They provide the platform, the template, the system of record, the general rules. They give you infinite customizability because they know customizations are necessary, but they do not step in and own each customer's business-specific operational layer. That is not their business.

On the other side are AI models and coding tools — powerful, flexible, and infinitely generative. But they do not own the system. They do not remember the account over time. They do not test in sandbox, deploy in production, monitor outcomes, or carry responsibility when something breaks. They help the user act. They do not own the action.

In between sit consultants, who implement the custom layer but cycle in and out. A year later, they are gone. Then another consultant comes in. Then another. Eventually you end up with fragmented logic, inconsistent documentation, scripts running in the middle of the night, and no one fully knows why.

### The Accelerating Macro Trend

This would already be a meaningful problem. But a more important trend is happening simultaneously.

Businesses are downsizing their internal operating teams. The same business complexity that required 100 people around the ERP may soon require 10. The ERP still matters. The business is just as complex. But the human operating layer is shrinking dramatically.

How are 10 people going to manage the same operational and financial complexity that previously required 100 people around the ERP?

### The Compressed Thesis

> NetSuite standardizes complexity, but does not own each customer's custom operational layer.
> Consultants implement that layer, but do not stay.
> AI models generate into that layer, but do not govern it.
> Meanwhile, the human teams around ERP are shrinking while the business complexity stays the same.
> **Helix exists to permanently own the operational layer in between.**

### The Core Distinction

**"Intelligence is not the product. Responsibility is."** *(Helix Manifesto)*

The future of AI is not suggestions or tasks. It is end-to-end ownership. Humans express intent. Helix owns outcomes.

## 3. What Helix Accomplishes

### The Boundary Test

Before describing what Helix does, it is important to establish what it does not do:

> **"If Claude Code can do it, it's not enough. If NetSuite can own it, we don't build it."** *(Helix Manifesto)*

Helix exists only in what neither side can own. It occupies the governed operational space between the ERP platform and general-purpose AI tools.

### Six Core Capabilities

**1. Autonomous Operational Lifecycle** *(Manifesto Principle: Completion)*

End-to-end handling of customization requests from natural language intent through governed production deployment, without requiring the user to manage intermediate steps. The user describes what they need. The platform investigates, plans, builds, tests, deploys, and maintains — taking full accountability for the outcome.

81% of all tickets execute autonomously. 47% reach governed production deployment.

Nothing Helix does is partial. Created, tested, deployed, verified. What Helix does is done.

**2. Governed Execution** *(Manifesto Principle: Safety)*

Every action is reversible, observable, and auditable. Trust is not assumed — it is engineered through safe testing environments, human approval gates before production deployment, and full audit trails. No change reaches a live business system without validated safety checks and explicit authorization.

This is fundamentally different from what general-purpose AI tools offer. They generate output. They do not govern what happens after.

**3. Production Inspection** *(Manifesto Principle: Calibrated Understanding)*

Read-only visibility into the live ERP environment to support diagnosis, monitoring, and proactive issue detection — without any risk to the running system. The platform understands the current state of the account before making recommendations, and monitors the state after changes are deployed.

Understanding enough to act, and acting enough to prove and refine the intent.

**4. Account Continuity** *(Manifesto Principle: Continuity)*

Persistent, durable memory of the account over time: what changed, what depends on what, what is fragile, and why decisions were made. This is the critical gap that consultants leave behind — fragmented knowledge spread across people who are no longer involved.

33 configured repositories across 7 organizations demonstrate multi-environment depth. The account lives inside Helix — not in scattered documentation across rotating consultant teams.

Helix does not complete tasks. Helix maintains systems.

**5. Bidirectional Communication** *(Manifesto Principle: Calibrated Understanding)*

Real-time interaction between the platform and human stakeholders, so intent can be clarified, decisions can be discussed, and accountability is visible at every step. When something matters, the platform shows up. When something breaks, it responds. When a request is important, it handles it.

**6. Enterprise Security** *(Manifesto Principle: Trust Through Behavior)*

Encrypted credential management, role-based access, and organizational isolation appropriate for handling production ERP environments that run entire businesses. Generating code is commoditized. Generating customizations is commoditized. Reliability is not.

Helix earns trust through completion, consistent behavior, and accountability for outcomes — not through intelligence alone.

### What Helix Is Not

> Helix is not a tool.
> Tools assist. Helix operates.
> Tools suggest. Helix executes.
> Tools are used. Helix is relied on.
>
> *(Helix Manifesto)*

Helix combines the ownership posture of a consultant with the speed of software. Tickets that used to take weeks can be resolved in minutes — without pushing responsibility back to the customer.

## 6. Competitive Landscape

### The Concentric Compression Model

The correct way to think about competition is not "who else does AI for NetSuite." It is two forces compressing from opposite sides, with Helix occupying the governed space in between that neither side wants to fully own.

> **"Capability is abundant, governance is scarce."** *(Reality Check & Risks)*

### Force 1: ERP Platforms Compressing Inward

**Oracle/NetSuite** is making the platform smarter — and moving faster than initially assumed:

- **NetSuite Next** (rolling out mid-2026 in North America): agentic workflows, Ask Oracle conversational intelligence, AI Canvas scenario planning, Redwood UI redesign
- **SuiteAgents** (announced SuiteWorld 2025): developers and users can build autonomous agents on SuiteCloud that "analyze your business and take action on your behalf"
- **AI Connector Service** (launched August 2025): connects NetSuite to external AI platforms including Claude, ChatGPT, and any MCP-compatible system
- **NetSuite 2026.1**: AI across financial close, reconciliation, forecasting, and developer tooling

*Sources: Oracle/NetSuite public announcements, validated via web search April 2026. [Evidence Tier: B]*

**Why this does not close the gap:** Oracle's business model is platform licensing and extensibility. They enable customization — they don't take end-to-end accountability for each customer's custom operational layer over time. Customers still rely on partners, consultants, internal teams, and scattered documentation precisely because NetSuite does not persistently own that layer for them.

In fact, Oracle making the platform smarter actually *increases* the complexity of the custom layer around it. More features means more configuration, more customization, more things to govern. The operational ownership gap gets wider, not narrower.

### Force 2: AI Model Vendors Compressing Inward

General-purpose coding agents are increasingly capable at code generation, testing, and pull-request workflows. They can now read codebases, edit across files, run tests, and deliver committed code. The next phase includes broader autonomy, enterprise controls, and safer sandboxed execution.

**Why this does not close the gap:** These tools generate code. They do not govern its deployment into production ERP environments that run entire businesses. They do not monitor its impact. They do not maintain it over time. They do not carry durable memory of the account. And they do not take accountability when something breaks.

Even the AI companies' own documentation includes examples of agentic misbehavior in production contexts — deleting remote branches, exposing authentication tokens, attempting migrations against production databases. This is exactly why "generic AI model in production ERP" is still a governance problem, not just a capability problem. [Evidence Tier: B]

### The Unclaimed Center: Governed Operational Ownership

Neither ERP platforms nor AI model vendors are claiming:

- Safe ERP-specific testing in sandbox environments
- Governed deployment with human approval gates and full audit trails
- Production monitoring after deployment
- Ongoing maintenance and issue detection
- Durable, persistent memory of the account over time
- End-to-end accountability for the custom operational layer

That is where Helix sits. Not competing with Oracle on platform intelligence. Not competing with AI tools on code generation. Owning the governed execution layer that neither side wants to fully own.

### Other Market Participants (Different Motions)

**AI-native ERP replacements** — Companies like Rillet ($100M raised, 2025) and Campfire ($100M raised) aim to *replace* ERPs with AI-native alternatives. Different buyer, different motion. They validate that the market sees ERP as ripe for disruption, but they attack the platform itself rather than the operational layer around it.

**Traditional NetSuite consultants** — These are potential channel partners, not competitors. The distribution partner collaboration referenced in the Dovie projections suggests a channel strategy where consultants resell Helix rather than compete with it. The platform replaces the repetitive execution work consultants do, while consultants retain the relationship and strategic advisory role.

**Important note:** A feature comparison matrix is intentionally not included. No direct competitor offers autonomous, accountable ERP operational ownership. A feature matrix against Oracle or AI coding tools would be misleading — they solve different problems in different layers of the stack.

### Key Risks with Mitigations

**1. Oracle builds it**

*Risk:* Oracle/NetSuite ships native AI features that cover Helix's value proposition.

*Mitigation:* Oracle's business model is platform licensing and extensibility. They have structurally decided not to own each customer's custom operational layer — that's why the entire NetSuite consulting ecosystem exists. Even with NetSuite Next, SuiteAgents, and AI Connector Service, Oracle is making the platform more capable, not taking end-to-end accountability for how each account's custom layer evolves. Making the platform smarter increases the complexity of the custom layer, creating more demand for governed operational management.

**2. AI tools become sufficient**

*Risk:* Coding agents become capable enough that businesses trust them to operate directly inside production ERPs.

*Mitigation:* Capability and governance are different problems. Even as AI tools become more capable at code generation and testing, the governance gap remains: who deploys safely to production ERP environments, monitors the impact, maintains changes over time, and takes accountability when something breaks? AI companies are in the business of generation. Taking durable operational ownership is a different category.

**3. Zero revenue today**

*Risk:* The company is pre-commercial with no confirmed paying customers.

*Mitigation:* 5 NetSuite organizations are actively running production workloads. 264 tickets and 124 production deployments demonstrate real demand. Revenue is a function of commercialization timing (pricing, billing, contracts), not product-market fit. The pipeline is conversion-ready.

**4. Cost scaling**

*Risk:* The $50K/month flat cost assumption may not hold as AI inference, infrastructure, and support scale with customer count.

*Mitigation:* The current cost base supports the early growth phase. At $1,500/month per customer, unit economics provide room for cost increases. A cost-scaling model is developing alongside the business. First-tier economics are proven; second-tier will emerge from real usage data.

**5. NetSuite deployment maturity**

*Risk:* 31% NetSuite deployment success rate versus 84% for general deployments suggests the most critical pipeline is still early.

*Mitigation:* Deploying autonomous changes to a production ERP that runs an entire business is the hardest problem in this space. The general deployment pipeline demonstrates core platform maturity. The NS pipeline is the newest and most complex component, with the highest safety requirements. Improvement is expected as the platform accumulates experience with NetSuite-specific deployment patterns.

**6. Competitive pace from both sides**

*Risk:* Both Oracle and AI model vendors are moving faster than initially assumed. The middle ground gets compressed.

*Mitigation:* "Capability is abundant, governance is scarce." Both sides are compressing the obvious layers — platform intelligence and raw capability — but neither is claiming the governed operational ownership center. The defensible zone is specifically account-specific continuity, governed execution, monitored change, and outcome accountability. That zone is getting more valuable as both sides compress toward it.

**7. Time window compression**

*Risk:* The window to establish the governed ownership layer before it gets commoditized is shorter than it feels.

*Mitigation:* This is precisely why capital is needed now. At 3 months, the thesis is right and the surface area is noisy. By 6 months, shallow "AI for ERP" positioning becomes crowded and weak. By 12 months, the real split between native AI features and true ownership layers becomes visible. Early capital accelerates the transition from proving the concept to owning the category.

**8. Exit comparable unverifiable**

*Risk:* The Oracle/Next Technik acquisition price ($60M) used as an exit comp is not publicly disclosed.

*Mitigation:* Use directionally — Oracle has demonstrated willingness to acquire NetSuite ecosystem companies. Lead with market-based math: at ~$18M ARR by month 36, standard SaaS multiples of 5-8x yield $90M-$144M. The exit scenarios ($50M-$150M) are consistent with market-based valuation, not dependent on the unverifiable comparable.

## 10. Future Vision

### The ERP-as-Database Thesis

ERPs are going to go the way of databases. Databases are used everywhere, but nobody interacts with them directly anymore. ERPs will become the same — infrastructure with baked-in financial and legal rules that runs in the background. The interface between users and their ERP will be an AI-powered operational platform.

This is not a prediction about ERPs becoming less important. It is a prediction about ERPs becoming less *visible*. The complexity stays. The rules stay. The financial and legal framework stays. What changes is how humans interact with it — through an intelligent operational layer that owns the custom logic, the deployments, the monitoring, and the continuity over time.

Helix is positioned to be that layer.

### Multi-ERP Expansion

The structural gap — ERPs don't own custom layers, consultants don't persist, AI doesn't govern — is not NetSuite-specific. It exists across the entire ERP ecosystem:

- **SAP** — 440,000+ customers. Same customization complexity, same consultant dependency, same operational ownership gap.
- **Odoo** — Growing open-source ERP with rapidly expanding customization ecosystem.
- **Other ERPs** — Every ERP that allows customization creates the same three-way gap.

**Expansion path:** NetSuite beachhead ($600M conservative) to multi-ERP ($50.1B ERP consulting market). The structural thesis and the operational ownership model transfer — the governance layer is ERP-agnostic by design.

**Honesty note:** Multi-ERP expansion is aspirational. No technical readiness for non-NetSuite ERPs exists today. The immediate focus is owning NetSuite customization completely before expanding. This is consistent with the 6-month kill rule: "Kill breadth. Own one thing completely."

### From Change Engine to Operating Layer

The product must evolve from handling individual requests to being the persistent system-of-operation where account-specific logic, approvals, monitoring, and ongoing accountability live. This is the transition that separates a real company from a commodity wrapper.

The trajectory is clear in the milestone gates:

- **Today:** Taking requests and executing them safely (a change engine)
- **12 months:** Knowing the account over time — what changed, what is fragile, what depends on what (a stateful operator)
- **36 months:** Being where the owned operational layer lives — the permanent home of account-specific business logic, governance, and continuity (an operating layer)

### Pricing Expansion

The $1,500/month entry price is a land-and-expand strategy. As Helix proves deeper operational ownership and the account becomes increasingly reliant on the platform's persistent memory and governance:

- **Entry:** $1,500/month (current)
- **Expansion:** $5,000 - $20,000/month (consultant replacement value)
- **Headroom:** 3x - 13x above entry price

This creates a natural revenue expansion motion that doesn't require acquiring new customers to grow — existing customers increase spend as ownership deepens.

### The Urgency

> **"Your structural thesis is accurate. Your timing assumptions should be more aggressive."** *(Reality Check & Risks)*

The window is shorter than it feels. Oracle is erasing superficial whitespace fast. AI model vendors are erasing superficial capability fast. The only thing left is owned, governed, account-specific continuity. That is the lane.

At 3 months, the thesis is right. At 6 months, shallow AI-for-ERP positioning becomes crowded. At 12 months, the real split appears between native AI features and true ownership layers. At 18 months, trust and governance become the deciding factors. At 36 months, either Helix is a real system-of-operation or it gets compressed into tooling.

That is why capital is needed now. Not to build the idea. To become the category before the window narrows.
